The Road to Zion

Palmyra

By Brian McKibben

 

1

            “Look!” called out my wife, Megan from the front passenger seat.  “It’s the Pennsylvania state line!  Quick everyone, hold your breath!”

            This travel tradition was inherited from Megan’s family.  The “Welcome to Pennsylvania” sign standing by the side of the interstate marked another state down on our trip toward Palmyra, New York.  I tapped CTR ring the kids had given me as a birthday present on the steering wheel anxiously.  Again, I checked the clock on the dashboard for the umpteenth time and mentally calculated that there were only about six more hours left of driving till we reached  Palmyra for what I was determined would be the best, most memorable McCraig family church history vacation of all-time anywhere, and without the help of him!

            The trip odometer continued to slowly count the miles since we’d left our driveway early this morning.  With only a modicum of contention (“That’s my spot!”  “No one has a spot with their name on it.”  “Don’t put your stuff on top of mine!”  “Not everyone can have their stuff on top.”  “I’m by the window!”  “We’ll take turns sitting by the window”), we had settled into a blissful state of unity and harmony.  Megan was in charge of making sure the harmony didn’t get out of control.

            But, by my latest calculation, we were still running late.  To start out the best, most memorable church history vacation ever, we absolutely had to start the trip out right by staying at the best hotel, closest to all of the church history sites in Palmyra.  But since this trip had been planned at the last-minute (thanks to a phone call from a not-so-favorite former mission companion of mine), that hotel was completely full.  No vacancies.  Nada.  Why?  Because this week marked the start of the Hill Cumorah Pageant season.  Ergo, no rooms available.

            But, in a stroke of what I attributed to be divine intervention, the hotel in Palmyra had called late the previous night saying they’d had a cancellation, and said that we could have the room for the coming week if we arrived by 4:00 PM today.  Otherwise, they would go ahead and offer it to the next hopeful person on their waiting list.  But I had already decided: that room was as good as foreordained to be ours for the week!  Even if we had to elbow some other nice family out of the way, it had our name on it.  We were definitely going to make it in time, because after all, as I alluded to before, this was going to be the greatest Higgs-free (more on that later) summer spiritual pilgrimage ever taken by a family, and it had to start perfectly.  And, having awakened all the kids last night and warned them appropriately, I had moved our departure time up this morning a generous amount.

            However, there had been some type of mix up this morning with our youngest daughter Piper and her luggage.  Piper is our last primary girl, and today she was sporting pig-tails at odd angles, a bright orange shirt, and sitting way back in our minivan’s very last row of seats.  The luggage problem had been that Piper’s suitcase, which had been packed last night, was found empty this morning for some reason (maybe a mysterious case of sleep-unpacking?).  This had caused us to run a little late, so now I was rationing our meal times and bathroom breaks in order to make sure we didn’t end up having to stay at the backup venue for the week: the Possum Creek Camp Ground, a creatively named mud bog with no running water or electricity.  The imperfect place to stay would, after all, be the first mustache on my Mona Lisa of a vacation.

            Stealing a look into the rear-view mirror, I saw that my newly deaconized son, Easton, was winning the fat cheeks breath-holding contest currently taking place between him, his older sister Marianna, and Piper.  Easton was starting to grow taller and stronger, now, which was helpful this morning as he had been a big help to me hefting everyone’s suitcases out into the back of the van while Piper re-packed her suitcase as quickly as possible.

            I edged the cruise control speed up a notch as we crossed the state line into Pennsylvania, and all five participants in the breath-holding tradition (the exception being Lauren, our oldest daughter) expelled stale, lung-fulls of old, boring Maryland air and gasped for the fresher and much more exotic Pennsylvania air.

            Megan looked over at the speedometer and said, “You’re speeding again, Will.”

            “I know, I know,” I said.  I sighed in frustration and grudgingly let the needle fall back to the legal speed limit again, glancing at the round shield and red background of my CTR ring.

            “You’re not usually like this, Will.  First you come up with this all-of-a-sudden trip to Palmyra---” she started.

            “The Palmyra pageant only comes once a year,” I defended.

            “Then you get us all up in the middle of the night---”

            “The hotel called and said they had a cancellation and we had to be there by 4:00 PM in order to claim our room,” I defended again.

            “Then you were upset with Piper---”

            “Everyone was supposed to have packed their suitcase last night, and hers was empty,” I said.

            “I don’t understand how that could have happened...I packed it with her last night,” Mindy mused while looking out the passenger side window.

            “Beats me.  But it was empty this morning,” I said.

            Megan turned her attention back to me.  “Anyway, you aren’t usually this...”  She hesitated.
            “What?” I asked, inwardly wincing from the recognition in her voice.

            “It’s almost like you’ve gotten your annual call from...” she began, but again trailed off.

            I turned my head slightly in her direction with nervous eyes.

            “He did call, didn’t he?” she asked.

            I shrugged non-committally.

            “So that’s it.  You’re letting him get to you, again, aren’t you?” asked Megan, one eyebrow raised at me.

            I sighed.  She knew me.  I would eventually end up blaming this entire trip on him.  After all, who did he think he was?  He’d called me up out of the blue a few days ago bragging about how he’d been chosen to be Father Lehi in the Palmyra pageant; that he’d been thinking of me and my poor family; and that he thought we should come up to Palmyra where he would give us the best vacation ever by getting us into the hotel, personally giving us back-stage access to all the sites, and finally, he’d pull some strings to actually get us into the pageant.  I’d told him no thanks, hung up, and then decided that no Utah Mormon socialite was going to outdo me on my own coast.  I’d grown up out here in the East and had been to Palmyra several times.  I could do this as well as or even better than him.  I even knew about the ice cream place in Palmyra that was popular with pageant people.

            But, step one was to get us into that hotel.

            “Will, are you sure you got everything packed for Piper this time?” asked Megan, shaking me out of my teeth-grinding thoughts.  She was searching for something to feed our children in the now-empty plastic bags we’d hastily packed with every morsel of junk food we could find in the house last night (as I said, no time to stop anywhere along the way).  “We don’t want to end up with another suitcase full of forty-two pairs of socks again.”

            “Yep.  In addition to socks, she packed underwear, pants, shirts, a toothbrush, and a couple other things...oh yeah, shoes!” I announced proudly.  “I told her what to pack myself while I was carrying stuff out to the van with Easton.”

            Megan nodded tolerantly, then tilted her head dramatically to the side and asked, “Did Piper’s bag make it to the van?”

            I started to answer, then caught myself.  I didn’t actually know the answer to that part.

            “Piper?” I called into the rear-view mirror again.  I saw Piper still sitting in the back seat, the one she never ever wanted, but which she had vehemently insisted she sit in today, back with all the luggage.

            “What?” she answered back quickly, her eyes wide open.

            “Did your suitcase make it into the van?” I asked.

            Piper’s eyes darted to the stack of bags beside her, then back to me.  “Yes,” she croaked softly.  “It’s right here beside me.”  She gently patted the suitcase on top of the stack.

            “Is she feeling okay?” whispered Megan.  “She doesn’t sound right.”

            “I hope so.  She didn’t act like anything was wrong this morning,” I answered.  “Of course, it was pretty early, so it was still dark and everyone was sleepy.”

            “That reminds me,” said Megan.  “I have to call Jared now that it’s daylight.  I need to make sure he takes care of the cat today.”  Megan pulled her cell phone out and dialed.  “Hello, is this Jared?  Hi, Jared.  This is Megan from next-door...  Your neighbor...”

            Jared lived in the house next-door to us back at home.  He had wavy, cow-licked brown hair, bright brown eyes, freckles on both cheeks, and new permanent teeth that were still too big for his face.  He was always at our house, usually eating something from our refrigerator, like the pumpkin roll made special for me by my mother last Christmas.  Which I ended up missing out on.

            “No, not the neighbors in the green house,” Megan went on.  “We live in the yellow house...  No, the one right beside yours...  Yes!  The one with the cat...  Exactly, that’s why I’m calling.  You see, we had to leave earlier than we thought, so I wondered if you could go ahead and start feeding Angel today...  Who’s Angel?  Angel is our cat’s name, remember?...   Yes, it is a funny name...  No, he’s a boy cat...  Okay, so you’ll remember to feed him?  Just one scoop per day, remember?  And change his water, too?  Okay, Jared.  Thank you!  Bye.”

            “Five bucks says we come home to a dead cat,” said Lauren.  Lauren was going through a... “dark”...phase.  Her eyes tended to sulk behind thick layers of black eye-liner, and I hadn’t really seen her express an emotion other than “annoyed” (is that even an emotion?) for quite a while.  The only thing ever-present with Lauren anymore was her latest find from the library, usually a thick book about vampires or wizards, and this seemed to be what she was focusing on today there in the back seat behind me.

            I checked Piper in the rear-view mirror.  As the cat-whisperer in our family, any anti-feline sentiments were usually taken quite personally by her, so I expected her to react negatively to Lauren’s comment.  Fortunately, Piper was still calm, so I concluded that she must not have heard Lauren’s five-dollar wager.

            The miles ticked by through the green hills of Pennsylvania much too slowly for my growing anxiety, and as we neared the New York border, the kids started to get hungry and restless, having exhausted all food and whatever handheld games or doodle pads they’d brought with them.

            In an effort to stem the rising tide of whining in the back, I tried a diversionary tactic.  “Marianna,” I said.  “You’re in charge of keeping us all on track this week.  Where’s your schedule?”

            Marianna’s face lit up with glee.  Marianna was the bright ball of happy sunshine in our family.  Sometimes a little too bright.  She loved learning about everything and loved even more to tell us all about it.  She rustled through the backpack at her feet and pulled out a pink and purple notebook with a green, feathery pen attached to its front.

            “Monday,” she read from the first page.  “Drive to Palmyra.

            “Tuesday, we’ll visit the Martin Harris Farm in Palmyra, an ice cream place that Dad knows about, and the Peter Whitmer Farm in Fayette.

            “Wednesday, the E. B. Grandin Building, where the Book of Mormon was first published, the Sacred Grove, and the Hill Cumorah.

            “Thursday, do baptisms in the Palmyra Temple, and then watch the Hill Cumorah Pageant.”

            “And Friday, drive home,” Lauren finished for her.

            “Didn’t we also list some things to do in the van?” I asked.

            “Oh yeah,” she said, flipping the page.  “Piper’s safety briefing, sing the Articles of Faith songs, the books of the Book of Mormon song, all verses of ‘Follow the Prophet,’ and spot the first license plate from Utah.”

            “Piper,” said Megan.  “Can you remind us of the safety rules?”

            Piper was put onto her school’s safety patrol last year, and as a result, she had become pretty insistent on leading anything and everything to do with safety in our family.  That’s actually putting it mildly.  She was a safety nut!  Recently, she had run a fire drill at our home with a stopwatch, with all main floor exits blocked, forcing us all to rappel on bedsheets tied together from a second story window.  All of this at 2:00 AM in the morning.  When we reached her waiting in her safety patrol harness and plastic badge at the rallying point, she’d informed us that we had all just died a painful and fiery death due to being twenty seconds behind the escape time recommendation of Rufus the fire station dalmatian.

            “Um, I dunno,” Piper started.  “How about look both ways before crossing the street?”

            Piper’s sudden lack of enthusiasm for the subject caused all of us to stare in her direction.

            “Okay,” said Easton slowly, intonating the surprise that most of us felt.

            “I know something we should remind everyone of,” said Megan.

            “What?” asked Piper.

            “We need to make a new password,” said Megan, turning in her seat to look back at Piper.

            “Oh, yeah,” said Piper gloomily.  One of Piper’s favorite safety rules was that any grown up other than her parents had to have the family password or she would not go with them.  Period.  “Is the bishop still mad at me?” she asked.

            “No, Piper.  He dried out,” said Megan.

            “What happened?” asked Lauren.

            “A couple weeks ago, Piper wouldn’t let the bishop into our house when he came to pick up his church keys that---”  Here, Megan turned a scolding eye toward our son.  “---Easton had in his room for some reason.  Unfortunately, it was raining at the time.”

            “Piper was home alone?” I asked.

            “No,” said Megan.  “Lauren was there, but she was upstairs reading with headphones on and didn’t hear the doorbell.  She missed the whole thing.”

            “Why did Easton have the bishop’s church keys, anyway?” asked Lauren, clearly trying to stir something up.

            “Hey, he asked me to go unlock a door for someone and I forgot to give them back to him,” said Easton, holding his hands up in surrender.  Absconding with the bishop’s church keys sounded like a feat Easton might like to pull off if only to prove it could be done.  Everyone knew that, so he was understandably trying to protect his honor.

            “Anyway,” said Megan.  “We need a new password, now, since we had to give the last one to the bishop.”

            “You mean ‘Fried Boogers’ is dead?” asked Easton.  “Aw, man!  That was a good one.  I liked it.”

            “Of course you did.  You came up with it,” said Lauren.

            “Anyone have a suggestion?” I asked.

            “‘My underwear’s too tight!’” Easton replied with a mischievous grin.  He came up with it very fast.  Too fast.  So I surmised that he had a list already populated with these types of pass-phrases in his head.  Everyone started laughing.  I started to disagree but Megan shook her head.  I just hoped I would never need to give it to anyone.

            “Okay, then.  The new password is now ‘My underwear’s too tight.’  What’s next Marianna?” I asked.

            “The Articles of Faith Songs,” she answered brightly, making a check mark by that entry in her notebook.

            “Piper, have you finished learning all the Articles of Faith in primary, yet?” asked Megan.

            “Not yet,” answered Piper.  She jerked her head over toward her packed bag, again.  I thought for a second she might be about to pull out the scriptures I had instructed her to pack that morning.  That way, she could find the Articles of Faith and sing along.  However, something about her jump told me that maybe she might be hiding something else in her luggage.  I wondered if I were jumping to conclusions, or whether I really should be suspicious.

            “I’m not singing,” said Lauren flatly, bringing her book back up in front of her face.

            “I think I’m too hungry to sing,” said Easton, his hand dramatically rubbing his stomach.

            Megan reached down under the passenger-side seat and fished around by her feet again and, this time, found a small box by her right foot.  It had a few remaining chocolate covered cake treats left in it.

            “Sorry, guys, there’s nothing left, after these,” said Megan, tossing the snacks back to each of our kids.

            “Hey, no interceptions allowed!” yelled Lauren, the prospect of food rousing her from behind the book.

            “I wasn’t intercepting.  I just didn’t want it to fall onto the floor,” yelled Easton from the other side of the van, with only a little sugar-coated indignity in his voice.

            “Mine is a little bit crushed,” said Marianna, peeling off the plastic wrapper carefully so as not to waste any of the caloric content in her rations.

            “Do I get one, Mom?” said Piper’s tiny voice from the back.

            “I passed four of them back there.  Where’s the fourth one?” asked Megan, her eyes on Easton.

            “Oh, here it is!” said Easton.  In the rear-view mirror I saw Easton suddenly “find” the fourth snack cake in the seat pocket in front of him.

            While the kids argued over who had the biggest last meal, Megan turned to me.  “Will, I can’t control them much longer.  Hunger has taken over.”

            “Okay everyone,” I said over the commotion in the back.  “Contest time!”

            “Contest?  What contest?” asked Easton.

            “Let’s see who can be the quietest the longest,” I said.

            “That’s so stupid.  It’s not even worth playing because you don’t actually win anything,” protested Lauren.

            “How about if the winner gets...” I reached back and pulled my wallet out.  “All the money I have in my wallet at this very moment?”

            My ploy worked.  The arguing stopped.

            “How much is in your wallet?” asked Marianna, voicing the obvious question.

            Now, I knew that I never carried actual money around with me.  I had converted to using nothing but plastic years ago.  But in order to push my bluff forward, I shrugged and answered, “You’ll have to play to see, won’t you?”

            I smiled and looked over at Megan, intent on giving her the “I am the master” face.  However, her face was wide-eyed and countering with an “I put money in your wallet without you knowing it” face.  My face then, of course, fell.

            “You put money in my wallet?” I whispered to her.

            “There was a bunch of money in the glove compartment, so I put it in there so it wouldn’t get lost,” she whispered back.

            “That was for the tollway in New York!” I whispered back.  “Now we’ll have to go a different way.”

            “The tollway should take credit cards, right?” she asked.

            “I have no idea,” I said.  I adjusted the GPS to bypass toll roads.  It put us even more behind schedule.

            And with that, all McCraigs in the van became silent.  However, unbeknownst to most of the McCraigs in the van, there was yet another family member present who would not be winning the money my wife had so inconveniently placed in my wallet.  Just as the quiet game had gotten underway, we suddenly heard a very distinctive sound from the direction of Piper’s seat:

            “BLEAAAYECGH!”

            The sound of highway zipping below us mixed with the drone of the engine was the only thing that followed the unmistakable sound that had interrupted the onset of the quiet game.  I knew that sound.  It was an all-too-common sound at our house.  Jared probably knew that sound, as well, he’d been over at our house so much.  I hated that sound.  And from the confused looks that I could see in the rear-view mirror, everyone else knew what that sound was, too.  And, it wasn’t anything lovely or of good report, or praiseworthy, either.

            “Um, what was that sound?” I asked calmly.  Why even ask?  As I said before, everyone already knew exactly what it was, and what had made the sound.  The only question was, why was it here in the van with us?

            More of the sound of the highway interspersed with the ticks of road cracks passing underneath.

            Finally, Easton raised up from his seat and peered into the way-back seat where Piper was sitting, staring in my direction with wide, unblinking eyes.

            “Uh-oh,” came Easton’s voice from below the rim of his seat-back.

            “What do you mean, ‘uh-oh’?” Megan asked.

            Marianna and Lauren both decided that anything that elicited an “uh-oh” from Easton was worth, at least in Lauren’s case, putting down her book and taking a look.

            “Oh no!” went Marianna.

            Piper, however, remained motionless and silent.

            “Who’s idea was this?” called out Easton.

            “Who’s idea was what?” Megan asked again.

            Now, honestly, I really did, as I said, already know what the answer to my earlier question was.  I knew what that sound had been.  But only the purest optimism in me urged me onward in my determination to persist in ignorance.

            “Whose idea was it to bring the cat!” clarified Easton.

            “The cat!?!” Megan exclaimed.  “The cat is supposed to be at home being cat-sat by Jared!  Did it just throw up?”

            “Sounded like it to me,” said Marianna.

            “Looked like it to me,” said Easton.

            And, at that point, our cat Angel began meowing loudly from the floor under Piper’s feet.

            Well, there it was.  No more optimism possible on my part.  “Who brought the cat?” I asked as calmly as I could, even though I was sure who the culprit was.  I was trying to decide whether it would be worth it to turn around and drive Angel back home from this point, now having crossed the entire state of Pennsylvania.  It would mean losing the hotel room and being forced to stay at the Possum Creek Camp Ground, the land of portable toilets.  I had to concentrate on my CTR ring at that point.

            Piper’s face began to contort into a sob and she reached down to pick the cat up.

            “I didn’t want Angel to feel left out!” she cried.  “He should know about the Church, too, right?”

            Great.  Wonderful.  How do you scold a kid for something like that?  I wonder which of the older kids had advised her to defend herself by giving that answer.

            “I won!” cried Lauren.

            “Won what?” I asked.

            “The quiet game,” said Lauren.

            I had to take a deep breath at this point.  I handed Mindy my wallet and looked away so as not to see how much money I’d just lost.

            “Wow, that’s a lot of cash,” said Easton as Mindy passed Lauren’s winnings to her.

            “Cha-ching!  Seventy-five bucks!” said Lauren, clearly enjoying the mayhem that was just now coming to a good boil.

            “Piper!” I spluttered as I turned on the right blinker for the next exit so I could pull over and assess the cat damage.

            “I bet he never has to worry about cat sick in his vehicle!” I grumbled to myself.

I slowed the minivan to a stop on the side of the off-ramp.  Before getting out, I took a deep breath, checked the clock on the dash again, looked over at Megan and tried to express with my eyes, “I’m counting to ten, I’m taking a breath, I’m doing my best to not lose my temper...but back me up!”  Then I stepped out of the van and went around to open the back gate.

            Yep, it was all true.  There was Piper, holding our gray and white cat Angel, who had just, with poetic irony, thrown up on my suitcase (who else’s would it have been?).

            I rubbed my forehead slowly.  One more look at the time.  Would it really be so bad to just take Angel along?  After all, how much trouble could a single house cat cause on a vacation to church history sites in Palmyra?

            “I guess we’ll have to stop and get some food for him,” I grumbled, reaching up to close the back gate.

            Piper’s eyes suddenly lit up.  “Oh!  That’s okay, daddy!  I brought food for him!”

            “You did?” I asked, looking around the pile of wedged-in suitcases for a cat-food container.

            “I didn’t want you to have to worry about Angel, so I packed his things in my suitcase,” she reassured me, trying to restrain Angel as he struggled to escape from her hug.

            “So, where did you put your own clothes?” asked Lauren, making a big show of counting ten dollar bills in her hand.  Apparently, she could guess where this conversation was heading, and was more than willing to stoke the chaos.

            Piper’s face fell.  I simply closed my eyes.  I suddenly realized why Piper’s suitcase had been unpacked this morning.

            “I didn’t have room for Angel’s food with the clothes still in there, so I took them out,” Piper said, reaching for the zipper of her suitcase to show me.

            “Wait!” I yelled.

            I wasn’t fast enough to stop her.

            As if in slow motion, I watched helplessly as reddish-brown kibble began to pour from the gap Piper had just unzipped, spilling all over the floor of the van, mixing in with the previous contents of the cat’s stomach, and out the back onto the ground.  And as I’d stepped forward in my effort to prevent the spill, I lost my footing and fell into the mess.

            “You mean you filled your whole suitcase with cat food?  After we had repacked it this morning?” I nearly cried, scraping slimy cat food off my jeans as I stood back up.  I took another deep breath, squeezed my eyes shut, closed the back gate (it took a few tries given that cat food had gotten into the frame of the door), and walked slowly back to the driver’s seat where, thankfully, Megan had a wet towelette for me to wipe the cat ick off my hands.

            “Here’s how it’s going to go,” I said as I wiped.  “Marianna, add this to the schedule.”  Marianna lowered her green-feathered pen and began scribbling in her book.  “Lauren, you are going to search on your phone and find out where the nearest Super Big-Mart is and guide us there.  Megan, make a list.  We need a cat carrier, cat food, cat litter, a litter box, bowls for the cat’s food and water, and some clothes for Piper and me.  The rest of you, pick your favorites from the list, because we’re going to divide and conquer at Super Big-Mart.  We’re running late as it is, and I don’t want to think of what will happen to that cat if we don’t make it in time to get our hotel room this evening!”

            And with that, staring at four dazed faces (including Angel’s, but not Lauren’s) in the rear-view mirror, I pealed out from the side of the road, leaving the pile of cat food I’d slipped in behind us, and sped back onto the Interstate.  Lauren started lazily swiping her cell phone while Megan arranged who was going to run and get what when we got to the store.  After about twenty minutes, Lauren guided us into the parking lot of a store in rural New York where we all (except for Easton, who would stay in the van with the cat, because as Piper insisted, “We can’t leave an animal alone in a van!”) bailed out and started running for the sliding front doors.

            “Why can’t I stay with the kitty?” asked Piper.

            “Because we have to get some clothes for you,” Megan answered.  She then took out the shopping list and reminded everyone of their assignments: “Okay, Marianna, you go and get two pairs of shorts and one pair of long pants for Piper, and three each of socks, shirts, and underwear.  Got that?”  Marianna nodded quickly.  “Lauren, run and get some chips, donuts, juices and whatever else looks good since we won’t have time to stop for anything else till we get there.  Dad and I will go and get the cat things.  Everyone meet back here in fifteen---”  I cleared my throat in Megan’s direction in order to remind her that we had to make it to the hotel in Palmyra on time.  “Er---ten minutes.  Go!”

            And we were off!  Marianna raced off in the direction of little girls’ clothing with Piper stumbling to keep up with her.  Lauren took off toward the junk food.

            Megan and I loaded up the cart with everything needed to keep a cat alive for a week.  Then, we knocked a couple of old ladies’ carts out of the way as we escaped from the pet aisle and barreled our way down the main concourse to our meeting place.  Lauren showed up with eight varieties of chips, one box of messy powdered donuts, and two huge jugs of juice.  I guess we hadn’t specified that little individual juice bottles for everyone would be best---we’d all get to share the love in the van!

            After a few more minutes of finger-tapping, sighing, pacing, and time-checking, Marianna finally ran up to us, alone, with an armful of Piper-sized clothes, and out of breath.  “Made it!” she panted.  Though the fact that she had arrived noticeably Piper-less hadn’t escaped the rest of us, she herself seemed blissfully unaware of this obvious flaw.

            We stood there for a few seconds, all of us looking at her expectantly as she caught her breath.  Was she going to fill us in on why she had arrived without Piper?  Or was she expecting us to guess?

            “What?” she asked, finally noticing that we were all looking at her expectantly.

            My eyes grew just a teensy bit wider at this question and Megan quickly rescued the situation.

            “Marianna, where’s Piper?” asked Megan.

            “I thought she was with you!” said Marianna.

            “Marianna, how could you get clothes for Piper without her?  How would you know whether anything fit her?” asked Megan.

            “Oh!  That’s okay, I got belts!” Marianna announced proudly, holding three belts up.

             “And why are all the shirts orange?” asked Megan.

            “I thought Piper liked orange.  Anyway, everything should fit,” said Marianna.

            “But Piper followed you!  Where is she?” I finally asked.

            Marianna’s brow furrowed in concentration and she bit her lip.  “Um, gee, I don’t know.  I didn’t know she followed me.”

            “Great!” I grumbled, throwing my stuff into the cart.  “Marianna, if we don’t get to the hotel in time this evening, no bathroom privileges for you for a week!” I hyperbolized.  “Everyone, throw your stuff into the cart here.  Watch the clock on the wall up front.  At the five minute marks, come back here and check in with Mom.  Megan, stay here with the cart.  Everyone else, split up and start looking for Piper.  When you find her, bring her back here!”

            “Did you leave the van unlocked?” asked Megan as Marianna and Lauren took off.

            “It locks itself about 30 seconds after the last door closes.  Why?” I asked, nervously checking the clock.

            “I just wondered if she would go out to look for us there,” she said.  “Probably not, though.  I think the safety patroller in her would tell her to find store security.”

            The following day, at the conclusion of our next church historical site visit, I found myself in the main foyer of the E. B. Grandin building, one of the hallmarks of church history, the place where the Book of Mormon was first published, and I was watching a flying, frenzied, feline ball of fur yowl its way around the room in front of dozens of onlookers I’d gotten to meet throughout the morning at one mishap after another.  The amazing thing was, I was not only laughing, I was absolutely jubilant!

B. Grandin Building, where the Book of Mormon was first published; then the Sacred Grove; then, if there’s time left over, we can go see the Hill Cumorah.”

Grandin Building, the Sacred Grove, and the Hill Cumorah.  Carrie-Willow didn’t have to draw a map this time, because apparently the hotel was stocked with a supply of pre-printed maps today.  I walked back to the elevator pretending to look at the map.

B. Grandin building, then chanced another quick glance back at Carrie-Willow.  One of the things I’d thought about accomplishing during this vacation was to give a Book of Mormon to someone, even if it were only a “check the box” kind of give-away.  But given Carrie-Willow’s violence (at least toward the poor keyboard) in her drive to help out someone in need, maybe she might be a good person to give a Book of Mormon to.

B. Grandin Building --- Site of the first publication of the Book of Mormon.”  More of the same plastic caution tape created a winding maze up to the last row of chairs under the tent for people to be admitted to the building in manageable groups.

            “What?” I asked, pretending that I hadn’t just abruptly halted tapping my feet nervously.

            “Everything will be fine.  Just enjoy the tour,” she said.

            I took a deep breath to clear my head and answered, “Okay, I’ll try.”  I took a look around to size up just who we would be offending today---otherwise known as our tour group.  We seemed to be in the middle of a large group of purple T-shirts.  Off to our left was a middle-aged guy wearing a yellow visor eating m&m’s and laughing at a joke he was telling to the purple T-shirted people in the seats in front of him.  There was a small boy swimming in a purple T-shirt fidgeting around in those seats who apparently was named “Tony,” judging from the number of times I heard “Tony stop bouncing, please.”  A group of young men and women ahead of them, all wearing the same purple T-shirts, appeared to be a youth group, all of them with digital cameras and cell phones clicking and flashing.  One of the older purple-shirted youth had a missionary name tag on his chest.

            As I panned backwards, I had to do a double take on my own kids.  They were just...sitting there.

            “What’s wrong?” Megan asked.

            “Huh?  Oh, nothing, I guess,” I whispered back to her.  “But our kids are acting pretty suspiciously.”

            She peered around me and looked down the row, then back to me.

            “Hey!” I yelled.  Everyone halted what they were doing and stared straight into my eyes in the mirror.  “This is not how we should be acting on the Hill Cumorah.”  I explained calmly.

Then I felt the van go ker-thump.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

11

            "Daddy, you just killed a little squirrel!" shrieked Piper.

            “What squirrel?” I shot back, simultaneously stomping on the brake.

            “The pretty black and white one,” Piper sobbed hysterically.  “Daddy, you have to stop and take it to the animal hospital we saw.”

            I quickly went through all the species and sub-species of squirrel I had ever learned about in my life: red, gray, flying, and secret.  Black-and-white was not in that list.

            “You mean gray?” I asked, puzzled.  After all, black plus white equals gray, right?  Maybe Piper was confused?

            By now, all the kids had turned their attention from the cat hair duct tape ball to something lying on the pavement behind us.

            “Uh-oh,” said Easton.

            As I have stated before, it never portends good when Easton says this.  “No, I think she means exactly what she said, Dad,” he said.

            I pulled over, put the van into park, unbuckled my seatbelt, and opened the door to go investigate this new species of squirrel.  One of the sayings I have heard repeated from time to time in church settings is, “You don’t have to see to know.”  This, of course, refers to spiritual things.  There are, similarly, things in this temporal life that also adhere to this saying.  For example, the sound of Angel throwing up didn’t require me to actually see in order to know what had happened on our drive to Palmyra.  And in this case, as soon as I opened the van door, I knew what we had hit, and exactly what a black and white squirrel really was.  And I didn’t have to walk all the way to the back of the vehicle to see it.

            “What is that?” Megan asked, her hand evidently clenched over her nose, judging from the nasal sound of her voice.  Megan had grown up as a city girl.

            “Apparently,” I gagged out, “It’s a black and white squirrel.”

            I gingerly made my way to the back of the van and peeked around the back tail light to see the lump on the road.

            “Is it still alive?” Megan called out.

            “Can you please hurry up?” Lauren called from inside the van, apparently also holding her nose, as well.

            “I just want to make sure the ‘squirrel’ isn’t going to do anything to fight back,” I called.  The lump on the road was, as Piper had correctly described, black and white.  Fur bristled in the gentle breeze which, unfortunately for me, was blowing past the black and white lump in my direction.  The lump wasn’t moving.  Luckily for us, it looked like a trip to the animal hospital would not be necessary.

            “I think it’s...” I started, but then, remembering Piper’s tender feelings on such matters, quickly made a mid-sentence correction, “It’s...uh, in the Celestial Kingdom, now.”

            I started back to the driver’s seat door and was just about to get in and drive away to cleaner air when Megan asked, “Are we going to just leave it there?”

            I nodded, as if to say, “You bet your smelly boots, we are.”

            “But, daddy, we have to take it to the animal hospital.  Maybe they can save it!” Piper cried.

            I looked back at Piper with the saddest eyes I could muster.  “Piper, I’m not sure how to say this---”

            “It’s dead.”

            Yep.  Lauren.

            “We have to do something with it.  It will smell up the entire pageant,” Megan said.

            For many years, now, I have been writing a book in my head.  I started it soon after Megan and I were married.  It has become my life’s work, my masterpiece, my magnum opus.  Its title: Spousal Translation Dictionary: Wifese-->Husbandese.  (Honestly, is there really any demand for a dictionary going in the other direction?)  In this dictionary, under the heading of the word “we,” here is what the entry looks like:

Wifese

Husbandese

we

(pron.) - you.

Example: “We should mow the yard” would be correctly translated as “You should mow the yard.”

 

            So, when Megan said, “We have to do something with it,” I mentally flipped through my Spousal Translation Dictionary, found the right entry, and correctly translated her utterance as, “You have to do something with it.”

            I wrinkled my nose up trying not only to stifle the smell, but also trying to decide what to do.  Suddenly, it occurred to me.

            “Easton!  Get your shovel and let’s go bury it!” I coughed through my still-open door.  Piper burst out crying.

            “I don’t want to touch that thing with my shovel!” he called back.

            “If we have to, we’ll buy a new shovel.  Get out here!” I said, finally relenting and pinching my nose closed, too.

            Easton exited the van, to the coughs and “ews” of all our womenfolk.  He took the long way around the front of the van and met me, peeking around the same driver’s side back tail light I had previously hid behind, and held out his fold-up shovel to me.

            “Thanks,” I said without any sincerity.  I unfolded it out to its longest possible length and started back toward the black and white lump on the road, my whole family watching me while literally holding their breath.  As I reached the spot where the unfortunate animal lay, I gently nudged it with the shovel and backed away quickly, just in case it was only playing possum.

            It remained motionless.

            Summoning up all my remaining bravery, I crept out from behind my protective tail light and tried to work the shovel under the hairy mound.  I quickly discovered that the lump was just too big to fit on the blade of the little fold-up shovel.

            “Megan, do we have a bag or anything that we can put this into?” I called to her.

            “We aren’t bringing that thing into the van, Will!” she answered back, triggering another instant translation in my head.

            “That’s not what I meant.  It won’t fit onto the shovel.  I need some way to carry it off the road.”

            “Just kick it off with your foot,” she suggested.

            “I don’t want that smell on my foot, either,” I countered.

            I heard some rummaging in the van.  Apparently, the greater good was in everyone’s nasal self-interest, so everyone inside the van cooperated to find a Super Big Mart plastic bag, which Easton brought back to me, reaching it to me from behind the tail light.

            “You hold it open while I shovel it into the bag,” I directed.  Easton slowly walked over to the head end of the lump and held the mouth of the plastic bag open, fighting back his gag reflex.  This, of course, left me on the business end of the dearly-departed animal, so I gingerly pushed and scraped the black and white fur-pile into the bag.  Just then, I saw a car at the top of the hill turning the corner to come down from the upper parking lot.

            “Megan, can you take the van down to the visitor’s center?  Easton and I will catch up in a few minutes,” I called.  Megan nodded and moved quickly around over to the driver’s seat, shifted into gear and sped away.

            “C’mon, Easton,” I said, pointing with the shovel toward the woods on the uphill side of the road.  “Let’s go bury this or the smell is going to be here for weeks.”

            Easton, carrying the bag with his arms outstretched in front of him as far as Easton-ly possible, followed me up the hill a little way, far enough from the road to be inconspicuous.

            “I’ll dig a little hole.  Wait for me to say it’s ready before you drop it in, okay?” I asked Easton.  He nodded and I set to work digging the hole.

            Digging with that little camp shovel was more difficult than I had thought it would be.  It was so short that I could get very little leverage with each turn, so it was taking longer than I’d hoped.  Eventually, I had a little mound of dirt beside a hole about a foot deep, which I figured would be deep enough to be an air-tight seal.  I stood up to stretch my back and was about to have Easton put his smelly bundle into the hole when I heard footsteps coming through the woods behind me.

            “Hey, who’s over there?” came a stern voice which, unfortunately, I recognized.

            “Uh o---“ started Easton.

            “Will you please stop saying that?” I hissed.

            The footsteps behind me quickened and before I knew it, Officer Duke was there by my side.  Again.

            “What are you two doing here?” he asked roughly, looking down at the hole.  Suddenly, though, he caught a whiff of our bag and, with watering eyes, asked “What is that?”

            “A skunk,” I answered simply.  Was he a city-kid, too?

            Officer Duke then turned his eyes up to me and recognized me.  “Hey, aren’t you Dr. Higgins’ personal assistant or something like that?”

            “Absolutely not!” I shot back, then calmed down quickly.  “Higgs was one of my missionary companions.  That’s all.”

            Officer Duke paused, looking from me to the hole, to Easton, and finally, to the bag-o’-skunk Easton was holding.

            “Well,” he said tentatively, evidently not sure what to do.  I could see in his eyes that he knew something was wrong, but apparently me being one of Higgs’ acquaintances threw the whole universe of Church security into a lawless wasteland of confusion, anarchy, and uncertainty.  “What’s this hole for?” he eventually asked in Easton’s direction.  Why?  I’m not sure.  After all, I was the one holding the shovel.

            “It’s for the skunk,” Easton answered, holding out the plastic bag for him to investigate.

            “No, no, that’s alright,” Officer Duke said quickly, turning his face away from the bag slightly.  “So what happened?”

            I sighed and explained how we’d accidentally hit it with our van on the way down the hill and were trying to get it buried before it stunk up the entire Hill Cumorah.

            “Probably a good idea,” Officer Duke reluctantly agreed.  “But you all shouldn’t be up here digging holes all over the place.  There are all kinds of power lines and water pipes running everywhere.”

            “You want us to leave it here?” Easton asked, proffering the skunk bag.

            To my surprise, Officer Duke answered, “Yeah, and you two go on.  I’ll take your package down over the hill to the dumpster and double bag it.”

            Easton handed the bag to Officer Duke.  He started down the hill while Easton and I went up to find the path to the visitor’s center.  Just as we found the path and started on our way around, we heard a sound from the direction Officer Duke had gone that stopped Easton and I in our tracks.

            Now, as I said before, some things in this life are immediately identifiable without the need for visual observance.  In this case, the sound we heard told us that I had not, as it turns out, killed the skunk with the van after all.  Piper would be quite happy to hear about that.  However, Officer Duke was going to need lots of tomato juice and baking soda for the next few days, as the “dead” skunk had been miraculously resurrected and taken its revenge out on him.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

12

            “So, where is this dance, anyway?” I said, watching Megan, Marianna, and Piper, all three arranged in a circle around a less-than-patient looking Lauren, fixing things about her hair and makeup which I obviously could not see.  Easton was sitting on a sofa off to the side, clearly unimpressed with all the girl-stuff going on.  But while Megan, Marianna, and Piper may have resembled the mice from the movie Cinderella, Lauren, on the other hand, reminded me of the word “tolerance,” but in an ice-pick-to-the-eyeball sort of way.

            “It’s in that stake center across the road from the Palmyra Temple,” Megan answered.

            “What do you think, Dad?” Marianna asked, bouncing with excitement as Piper began snapping pictures.  I would have guessed that she was supposed to be photographing Lauren, but with the camera pointed in some very strange orientations, I didn’t know what would actually come out.

            I checked Lauren from head to toe...actually, from toe to head; that way, I could conduct the exercise without being immediately scorched by Lauren’s gaze.  Shoes, jeans (not black), a pastel-colored T-shirt, hair pulled back.

            And then it occurred to me.

            “Wait a minute.  Where did these clothes come from?” I asked.  I knew full well that Lauren’s current wardrobe consisted of two colors: black and blacker.

            “Well...” began Megan, looking from Lauren to Marianna.  “Ray sent it over,” she chirped quickly.

            “Ray?” I asked, angling my head slightly.  I’d evidently heard her wrong, because, one, I didn’t think we knew anyone named “Ray,” and two, she said it a little too quickly, as if she didn’t want me to actually hear.  Therefore, I couldn’t just let it go.

            “Um,” paused Megan, standing up straight and smiling sheepishly, “You know, Raylene.  Higgs’ wife.”

            My eyes rolled back and I instinctively began counting to myself.

            Lauren took the opportunity to escape and plopped down unceremoniously from her dais.  “It all arrived on a private jet this morning from Utah,” said Lauren.  The worst part about this comment was that I couldn’t actually tell for sure whether she was telling the truth or just taunting me, because in this case, either option seemed plausible.

            Megan quickly grabbed the upside-down camera from Piper and handed it to Marianna.  “Make sure to take some pictures so we can send them to Raylene,” she said softly.  Then Megan took me by the arm and led me up out of the great room into the foyer, where the tuxedoed staff was just now wheeling out the last of the dinner dishes.  “Now, don’t ruin this.  It’s not a date.  It’s just a dance.  Lauren is...”  At this point, the customary word to describe the mood of most other young women would have been “excited,” but Megan chose a more fitting term for Lauren’s case, “...intrigued by the situation and I don’t want to keep her from getting a chance to experience a normal, teenage activity.”

            “What about all the dances she skips at home?” I protested.  Why, out of all the people to finally take an interest in, does she decide to go to a dance with the son of the guy that perennially drives me nuts?

            “Will!  There are four boys in our entire stake her age, and they don’t go to the dances, either,” Megan answered.  “So we are going to get a grip so we don’t cause a scene!”

            The Spousal Translation Dictionary again came into play.

            Lauren approached us from the foyer entrance followed by Marianna, Piper, and Easton.  Every now and then, the cosmos tells me to stop and take note of something special.  This was one of those moments.  Here we all were, our whole family together, in Palmyra, our oldest daughter finally experimenting with the visible light spectrum.  I figured it was a moment to relent and put my own feelings aside.

            “Marianna, let me see the camera,” I said, and then asked one of the staff members (who I’d counted was now on his fifth cart of dinner table stuffs) to take our family’s picture in front of a travertine fountain adorning one of the side walls in the foyer.

            “Now, you have fun, okay?” Megan said, cradling Lauren’s face in her hands as we moved toward the front doors of the Suite.

            “Yes, mother,” Lauren muttered through Megan’s grip.

            “Are you ready for me to take you over?” I asked, fishing the van keys out of my pocket.

            “Actually, I’m just going to walk over,” said Lauren, eyeing the keys suspiciously.  “It’s only half a block and there’s a walkway over to the building.  I’m meeting Hyrum...”

            I stifled a growl at sound of that name.

“...by the door, there,” she continued.  “Besides, I don’t want your reactions.”

            “Oops,” I thought to myself.  Guess I need to work on that part of parenting.

            And with that, Lauren floated out the door, where the ever present doorman bowed her through.  Marianna and Piper flew to the large window overlooking the lawn outside to watch Lauren on her walk over to the church building where the dance was taking place.  The sun was once again setting behind the hill where the Palmyra Temple stood.  Lauren appeared on the walkway below to the squeals of Marianna and Piper and a sigh from Megan.  Then she disappeared into a small copse of trees between our hotel and the church building.

            “Well,” I said, clapping my hands together.  I’d formed a plan in my mind.  Something needed to be done to make sure that this relationship with the enemy didn’t develop any further.  “I’m going to go see if I can get some ice.”

            “Ice?  I think there is plenty already in the freezer,” I heard Megan say as I hurried out through the ornate front doors.  I ran down the hallway and ducked into a stairwell, then down to the ground floor.  However, instead of going through the lobby as we usually did, I exited out through a doorway at the north end of the ground floor hallway this time, then jogged around in a different direction to avoid Marianna, Piper, Easton or Megan seeing me from the “Suite on high.”

            After a few minutes of creeping silently from the cover of one parked vehicle to another, I made it to the edge of the line of tall trees Lauren had just disappeared into.  A quick sprint through the trees put me behind a small brick outbuilding at the corner of the stake center’s parking lot.  From there, I risked a peek toward the main building to see whether I could find Lauren or not.  Knowing her, she was about to tap me on the shoulder and ask me what I was doing there.  In fact, I checked behind me, just to make sure.  Lucky for me, though, tonight she was “intrigued,” as Megan had said, and consequently a little distracted, and I could see her waiting under the porch overhang by one of the side entrances to the stake center.  She was pacing slowly around the porch, looking toward the road in front.

            I felt a buzzing in my back pocket, and reached back quickly to silence my phone.  It was a message from Megan: “You still looking for the ice?” it read.  I turned the display off and returned the phone to my back pocket.  I’d have to figure out how to deal with Megan later.

            Just then, a familiar yellow Hummer turned into the parking lot from the road and stopped in front of the porch where Lauren was waiting.  I imagined to myself the celebrity treatment the Higginses must be accustomed to at events like this:

“And look!  Arriving just now, it’s Higgsy Higgins, being driven by his father, the fabulous Dr. Hyrum LeGrand Higgins, of the Salt Lake City Avenues Higginses.  He’s wearing a dashing jet-black Armani tuxedo, no doubt picked up on their recent weekend stay in Milan.  Tonight, in true style, bringing to life the ‘prince and the pauper’ story, he’s meeting his ‘plus one’ for tonight’s gala, Lauren-someone-or-other, from the quintessentially common mission field.”

The passenger door opened and Higgs, Jr. hopped out.  He, too, was wearing blue jeans and an orange T-shirt.  Lauren stopped pacing.  Higgs, Jr. jogged over to where Lauren was waiting.  She said something to him quietly, then turned to survey the woods I’d just emerged from.  I ducked quickly back.  She might have been a little distracted, but she was still not someone to trifle with.  Higgs, Jr. laughed, pointed his thumb back at the yellow Hummer, and said something back to her.  They both laughed.  I tightened my jaw as he gave her a little one-armed hug and then trotted over to the door and opened it for her.

            The yellow Hummer drove off around the other side of the stake center.  Evidently, for that ostentatiously oversized vehicle, it was easier for Higgs, Sr. to warm the planet another couple of degrees by doing an entire circuit of the parking lot to get back to the road rather than back up and turn around.  I squatted down behind the outbuilding to wait for a few minutes.  Was I really going to go in?  What exactly would I do?  Follow them around?  Leave tacks on chairs?  Put a frog in someone’s drink?  I couldn’t just blend in with the crowd.  Honestly, I didn’t look young enough anymore.  I continued to brainstorm for a few minutes, but was unable to think of anything more concrete.  So, with spy movie soundtrack music playing in my head, I shrugged and headed in to see what I could see, hoping that perhaps an opportunity to sabotage this get-together would arise.

            Inside, I sidestepped along the church-standard abrasive, scratchy burlap walls, which were pulsating to the thudding bass of music coming from the gym.  I hugged a corner (scratching off a couple layers of skin on the burlap in the process) to check that the coast was clear.  Some kid with a blue “Proud to Be Crowd” T-shirt slurped a drink from a water fountain, then disappeared around the far corner.  I dashed down to a doorway close to the library.

            Modern Church buildings have something in common with Super Big Marts: wherever you go, when you walk into one of them, you already know where things are, because every one of them seems to be based on the same set of five or six floor plans.  Just as everyone knows where to find the boloney at Super Big Mart, whether you visit Ogallala, Nebraska or Pocatello, Idaho, you likewise already know where the bathrooms, library, and Relief Society rooms are in a modern Church building.  So, when I ducked into that room next to the library, I was happy to see that I’d found exactly what I was looking for: the room with “the door.”

            Every male who has grown up in the Church knows about “the door.”  (I was surprised to find out after fifteen years of marriage that my wife had no inkling that “the door” even existed.)  As an Aaronic Priesthood holder, when my ward’s building had grown too small to contain all the needed classrooms, our Deacon’s quorum was thrilled to be relegated to holding our priesthood class through “the door.”  The door I am referring to is the door which held the ladder or stairway to what I always termed the “Terrestrial Kingdom,” where all the ventilation and plumbing pipes were hidden above the ceiling.  Imagine the excitement of a twelve year old boy to be told by the Deacon’s quorum advisor, “Everyone up the ladder to class.  Take this rope, tie it off on a pipe, and let’s pull up a few folding chairs.”

            And, also due to my vast experience with the Young Men’s program searching for basketballs squirreled away on Sundays by “patient” bishoprics, I also knew the trick with a credit card to open “the door.”  I closed the door quietly behind me and dashed up the wooden stairs (bumped into a pipe, but the thudding of the music covered up the sound...of me yelling, “Ouch!”  After all, my head was still sore from my experience at the Whitmer Farm).

            It was mostly dark up there, with flashing strobes from the gym throwing long strips of light through vent grates along the pipe-strewn walls.  I paused at a vent grate and looked down into the gym.

            A mirror ball hung over the center and bathed the boiling mass of decidedly casual outerwear.  Down on the gym floor below, hundreds of kids, all with “Proud to Be Crowd” T-shirts lived up to their namesake and...well...crowded the floor.  One kid even had a colorful Lamanite headdress on.

            Finally, I found Lauren.  She was being led into the gym by Higgs, Jr. (him behind her, steering her in by her shoulders).  Lauren was definitely not in her element here.  Wide-eyed, her arms folded tightly, looking at everyone around her, she followed his direction through the rowdy throng toward a corner where several standard Church-issue round tables were set up.  He pulled a seat out for her at one of the tables, which, now that I noticed it, was decorated differently than the others.  It had a white tablecloth, a flower centerpiece, and two place settings.  Lauren sat down and crossed her legs to match her arms.  Then, Higgs, Jr. left her there!  Alone.  He just walked right out the side door behind her.

            “Whoa!  He can’t leave her there alone!  What’s he thinking?  That’s not how a man treats a lady!” I spluttered to myself.  I stood up straight, bumped my head on another pipe, then tripped over some dusty, insulated ductwork, and started back the way I’d come.  I was going to go rescue Lauren and leave with her so she wouldn’t have to sit there alone like that.  As I was thinking of it, it occurred to me that this meant I wouldn’t actually have to do anything in order to break them up.  I might even earn “hero dad” status in the process.  Perfect!

            Just as I was approaching the stairway to go down, I saw the stairwell light up briefly from below and then heard someone climbing the unfinished wooden stairs.  With wide eyes, I tip-toed as quickly as I could back toward where I’d been watching Lauren through the grate and ducked down behind the duct-work I had tripped over.  Who could it be?  Could someone possibly have seen me peeking through the vent grate?  Maybe whoever it was wouldn’t be able to see me amongst the pipes up there since it was so dark.

            Then it occurred to me, these attics usually had lights.  How silly was I going to look to whoever it was, squatting behind a pipe up here in the dark?  What if it were a chaperon?  Expecting the lights to come on at any moment, I risked a peek from behind the pipes I was hiding behind to see whether whoever-it-was was coming any closer.  The flashing lights from the gym provided transient glimpses of a man threading his way through the pipes in my direction, but I couldn’t see well enough to know who it was.  However, I could tell that he was stopping every so often to peer down through the grates, just as I had, onto the floor below.

            The fact that he was stopping every so often gave me a few more moments to plan.  The trouble was, I couldn’t think of anything better to do than simply find a place to scrunch myself in between more pipes.  Finally, I found a space on the floor where someone had abandoned a wadded up volleyball net.  Since it looked softer than the chipped wood subfloor prevalent everywhere else, I sat down on it and craned my neck up as high as I dared to risk another look in the intruder’s direction.  It appeared he was still looking down into the gym, so I decided to take another look myself to see what was going on with Lauren---after all, it had been a whole two or three minutes since I’d checked on her last!

            Lauren was still sitting at the table by herself.  So, could it be Higgs, Jr. was up here with me?  But that wouldn’t make any sense.  Why would he come up here just to watch her through a vent grate?

            I looked back at Lauren.  First, she had her hands folded in her lap.  Then she moved her hands to the table in front of her.  Then she shifted in her seat.  All the while, she was looking around from the pumping crowd on the gym floor to the door where Higgs, Jr. had left her a few minutes earlier.

            Finally, the door Lauren was watching opened and Higgs, Jr. returned.  That meant that the intruder up there in the Terrestrial Kingdom with me was not Higgs, Jr., after all.  This time, though, Higgs, Jr. was carrying a box, holding it above his head so the bouncing kids on either side of him wouldn’t knock it out of his grip.  He hurried over to the table where Lauren was sitting and sat down beside her.  He handed Lauren the box, which she opened.

            Out of the corner of my eye, I saw the person up in the attic with me move away from the grate he’d been looking through and head in my direction.  I ducked quickly.  The phone in my back pocket buzzed.  I took it out, cradled it in my hands to block any light from escaping, and checked.  It was another text from Megan: “Where are you?”  Figuring that “getting ice” was probably not going to work (and after all, it would be a lie, right?), I dashed off a reply, “Just taking a little walk.  I’ll be back soon.”  I had actually been walking, right?

            I peeked out again to see where the attic intruder was now.  He was watching through another grate a little closer to me.  Seeing that he wasn’t moving, I turned slowly back to look down toward Lauren again.

            The box Higgs, Jr. had carried in was laying empty on the table beside her and she was now holding up a handful of light blue flowers as if they were tainted with some sort of dire disease.

            Klunk!  I heard a noise off to my left.

            “Ow!” said the intruder.  I involuntarily jerked my eyes there and recognized, thanks to the voice, and to my annoyance, who it was that had come up the stairway after me.

            “Higgs?!?” I said.

            “Elder McGreg?” said Higgs.

            “What are you doing here?” I hissed at Higgs.

            He stepped over another air-conditioning duct toward me and fell nearly on top of me.  I pushed him back away from me a little more bruskly than might be considered polite.  He brushed off his pant legs, smiling as if nothing had happened.

            “Er, well...”  Higgs looked up at the ceiling some more, brushing dust off of his hands.  Finally, he looked at his expensive watch and sat down beside me.  “I was told it was a surprise.  Are you in on it, too?”

            “Apparently not.  Who is it for?” I asked irritably.

            Higgs paused for a second, then checked his watch again.

            “Well, here’s the story,” he began.  “I don’t know if you’ve noticed or not, but we keep ‘accidentally’ running into you all around the area!”

            “Yeah, I’ve noticed.  I think Lauren and your son have made sure of that,” I said.

            “Well, we’ve noticed that you seem to be having trouble with Lauren.”

            “Excuse me?” I bristled.  “Lauren is fine.  We don’t need---”

            “Bro, I don’t mean trouble like you don’t know how to take care of her.  I mean trouble as in she doesn’t want you to meddle in her life.”

            I gritted my teeth and stared straight ahead.  How dare he make a judgment about something that I’m smack in the middle of doing!

            But, darn it, he was right.  Lauren, as capable and independent as she was, still needed some guidance, but Megan and I could always sense a sort of “keep your distance” attitude from Lauren whenever we would try to offer advice of any kind.  We felt like we knew what course would bring her the most happiness, and we tried to steer her in that direction, but no matter how subtly we tried to do that, she always seemed to resent it.

            But now it was worse: even Higgs Higgins could tell.

            “Look, Bro,” he continued, “Ray and I thought about it and want to help you guys.”

            I sat there stewing.  What made him think that Megan and I didn’t know what to do?

            “We haven’t been exactly encouraging Higgsy to do anything, but, first off:  I think it’s safe to say that he is intrigued by your daughter---”

            There was that word again: intrigued.  It probably wasn’t a coincidence that Megan had used the same word.  Probably collusion between her and Raylene, was my guess.  Was everyone in on this except me?

            “Stop there,” I interrupted him.  “I don’t want to hear any more about that.  Is there anything else going on that I’m not in on?”

            A few minutes later, the pageant got underway, and the kids discovered that we could, in fact, see what was going on after all.  So they settled in under the blankets that we’d brought.  Before we knew it, Higgs was out there being Father Lehi with his family, prophesying doom and gloom in Jerusalem.  But I was having trouble paying attention.

            I really had so much wanted to be able to surprise Megan and the kids by pulling them into the costume department, donning some big, bright Book of Mormon garb, and getting destroyed with them at the foot of the temple.  Ah, wouldn’t that have been great?

            And then, on top of that, there was the problem with the Garrs, which I hadn’t even suspected was going on until just a few minutes ago.  I thought they were comfortably set up in the hotel room we’d given them.  And all that time, while we had been living in luxury (there, I admitted it, okay?), they had been slopping around in the mud all week.

            I got a sudden idea.  And I knew I had to act on it.  The kids wouldn’t be happy, and we’d miss out on seeing Carrie-Willow the next morning, but Megan would probably give me points for it.

            “Megan,” I whispered to Megan as Higgs started his vision of the Tree of Life.

            “What?” she asked, her eyes still watching the depiction of the vision.

            “I...”  I was battling in my head whether to tell her about the idea right then and there.  “...I think I’m going to check with the hotel about something.”

            “About what?” she asked.  “In the middle of the pageant?  Higgs is up on stage, now.”

            “Yeah, I know.”

            “But this is what we came for.  Don’t you want to see the show?” she asked.

            I smiled.  “I’ve seen him, I mean...the show before.  Besides,” I said, getting up from my seat as the lights dimmed for a change of scene.  “I know how this all ends up.”

            I hurried off to the tree line flanking the audience seating area, toward the Gadianton Bathrooms we’d passed on our way in, and pulled out my phone.  I dialed the number for the hotel and waited for someone to answer.

            “Hello.  You’ve reached the Palmyra Inn.  How may I direct your call?”

            “Hi, is this Willow?” I asked.

            “Yes,” she said.

            “Hi, this is Will McCraig.”

            “Dr. Higgins’ friend,” she said simply.

            “Yeah,” I said, shrugging.  “That’s right.  Hey, I wondered if by some chance there were a room open there tonight?”

            “Um, no, not really,” she answered.

            “Hmmm,” I said.  I looked over toward where Megan and the kids were sitting.  I weighed some options in my head.  If worse came to worse, we could simply start driving home and stay somewhere else tonight.  “I wondered if, for tonight, I could put another family into the room we’ve been staying?”

            “You want to put someone else into Dr. Higgins’ suite tonight?” she asked.  “Have you all checked out already?”

            “No, not really,” I said.

            “Then where are you planning to stay tonight?” she asked.

            “Honestly, I’m not sure.  But we’ll figure something out,” I said.

            “Let me guess: this is for that family you originally gave up your first room for, isn’t it?” she asked.  “I heard about that.  Is that it?”

            “Um, yeah, it is.  So, can we do that?” I asked.

            She hesitated and then said, “Well, here’s what I’ll do.  The room you were originally supposed to have is supposed to be ready later tonight.  How about if I put them into that one?”

            I looked back toward the pageant.  It looked like we were up to where Lehi and his family were building the ship.  I could see the Garrs watching in their wet, muddy clothes.

            “How about this?” I asked back.  “We’ll take that room and let this other family take the suite for the night.  Is that okay?”

            Carrie-Willow agreed and did some typing and then we hung up.  At least one of my failures for this trip was now resolved.  I headed back to where Megan and the kids were and sat back down with them.  I explained quickly to Megan what I’d done and she smiled her approval at me.

            After a little while, Lehi and his family finally arrived in the Promised Land.  Higgs gave thanks at an altar.  But as he did that, something small caught my eye on the far side of the stage.  It was gray and white, and was walking around with its tail held high in the air.

            It was our cat, Angel!

            And I wasn’t the only one who had seen him.  I looked over at my family, all of whom were now looking at me with wide eyes.  At the next scene change, I motioned for everyone to huddle so we could figure out what to do.

            “Dad!  Did you see Angel?” asked Piper.

            “Yes, I did,” I said.

            “How are we going to get up there to catch him?” asked Easton.

            I lowered my eyes and started thinking.  I was sure that if we didn’t take action now, we might lose our chance.  Who knew if we’d ever see him again once the destruction scene, with all the bedlam and chaos, took place.

            “Well,” I said.  “We have to do something, now rather than later.  Any ideas?”

            Megan looked at me mischievously, then turned to Lauren and asked, “Is that headdress you got at the dance still in the van?”

            Lauren nodded that she had, indeed, left her headdress from the dance the night before in the van.  Megan directed us to grab our blankets and head out.  We waved at Ed the usher as Megan led us toward Zion’s Camp.

            “Let’s do this,” said Megan, smiling at me.  “Will, take this blanket and wrap it around your shoulders.  Put on the headdress.  Piper, can we borrow your belt?”

            “My pants will fall down,” said Piper.

            “Nevermind about the belt, then,” said Megan.  “Where’s Angel’s leash?”

            Piper grabbed the leash from under one of the seats in the van.  Megan tied it around my waist.  Suddenly, I looked sort of like a cast member.  And since it was dark, maybe it would work.

            “You,” she said, pointing at Marianna, “Put this other blanket on.  Do we have any napkins or anything?”

            “In the box!” said Easton, kicking off toward the Higgins’ “box” (which I still say is really a shed) where the Higginses kept all their extra culinary supplies.  Easton came back with a couple of large, linen napkins along with some fancy ribbon.  Megan used those to make shepherd hats for Marianna and Easton.  She cut head-holes in the middle of another couple of our blankets and draped them over Marianna and Easton, then tied them off at their waist like long doublets.

            “Okay, then,” I said, marveling at Megan’s resourcefulness.  “Marianna, Easton, and I will make our way on stage to look for Angel.  Megan, Lauren, and Piper will look around on the sidewalks along the side.”

            “Dad,” interrupted Lauren, holding her phone to her ear.

            “What is it, Lauren?” I asked.

            “Hold on a second,” she said.  “Hyrum is going to meet us.”

            “Who is Hyrum?” I asked.

            “You know...Higgs, Jr.?” she answered.  “And, I’ve got this!” she said, holding up her honorary crowd lanyard.

            Lauren had pulled an ace out of her sleeve.  We made our way back over toward the stage where, sure enough, Higgs, Jr., dressed as a Nephite young man, was waiting for us by the Gadianton Bathrooms.  The combination of him and Lauren’s lanyard got us past the checkpoint and up the hill a little ways.

            “Do you know which level you saw your cat on?” Hyrum asked once we’d made our way halfway up the hill.

            “It looked like about halfway up, but we couldn’t tell for sure,” I said.

            “I have to go on stage here in a minute and throw things at Samuel the Lamanite.  Try looking up and down these paths that go along the side of the stage.  I’ll help once my scene is over,” he said.  Then he waved and left us there.

            We walked up and down, trying to remain inconspicuous.  There was a lot of activity going on.  Cast members and supporting crew were constantly moving back and forth, so we were able to blend in to some extent.  I kept my eyes on the stage itself while Megan and the kids tried calling out “Angel” every now and then while looking into the bushes.  For as long as I’d ever known, I had yet to see Angel respond to his name, so I wasn’t sure how much good that would do, but since it was the only tool in our belt (or, in my case, cat leash) at that point, we had to use it.

            Finally, on our third trip back down the hill, I looked over and felt my heart skip a beat---I’d found him!  Angel was sitting on the edge of one of the platforms, right next to a hidden stairway.

            The lights went down again between scenes.  I grabbed Marianna and Easton and whispered, “When the lights come up, find a place to fit in.  Angel’s right over there.”  And then we ducked down, ran onto the stage in the dark, hid behind a big thing, and waited for the lights to come back up.

            While we waited, some things started to come together in my mind.  If Higgsy had been headed out to throw things at Samuel the Lamanite, that meant we were coming up on the destruction scene.  And if the destruction scene were coming, whatever we were hiding behind was probably going to fall.

            Suddenly the lights came up.

            Samuel the Lamanite was just climbing up onto the wall with wide, outstretched arms when I tugged on Marianna’s and Easton’s sleeves.

            “We’ve got to hurry!  The world’s about to end!” I told them.  “Blend in with the crowd trying to kill Samuel and look for Angel.”

            As the real cast crowd moved past us, we separated and started following them.  I kept looking around, under platforms, behind props, anywhere that looked like a place Angel might hide, trying to find him there on the stage.  Not wanting to get too far from Marianna and Easton, I checked around for them, too.  Marianna was several feet behind me, smiling hugely, staring everywhere but where Angel might be:  at the audience, at the lighting towers on either side of the staging area, and around at other cast members.  Easton, similarly, had abandoned the search for Angel, but was actually succeeding at blending in much better than Marianna.  When the young men at the base of the wall started throwing things at Samuel the Lamanite, Easton was all in.  He picked up whatever he could, and chucked it up toward Samuel.  But while the real cast members were carefully aiming their throws in order to avoid hitting Samuel, Easton was totally into his part, and was aiming for the kill.

            “Easton!” I hissed.  He looked back at me.  “Stop trying to kill Samuel and do what you’re supposed to be doing!” I whispered.

            Finally, Samuel had had enough, and he hopped down and ran away.  The time had come.  The destruction scene was about to start, and we still hadn’t found Angel.  All around me, Nephite civilization began to break down.  And then the earthquakes and storms started.  The air filled with loud noises, screams, bright flashes of light, red lava spouting into the air, buildings falling on people, people falling off buildings, and Nephites dying all over the stage.  I looked over toward Easton, and then back at Marianna.  They looked like they were asking what to do.  I shrugged and fell down as dramatically as I could, hoping my death was a convincing one.  I squinted down the hill and could see the Garrs in the front row again.  Their moment was almost here.  Sister Garr’s glistening eyes were taking in all the carnage, her hands clasped in nervous anticipation in front of her chin.

            And then total darkness fell over the entire Hill Cumorah.  A voice proclaimed, “The Savior has died.”  Somewhere down below us, I could hear someone with great spiritual sensitivity sniffling into a tissue.  (I suspected it was Sister Garr.)

            Then there was silence as deep as the darkness.  I looked up at the stars now visible in the night sky.

            From the pageant speakers, a gentle, deep voice spoke into the darkness, “Behold, my Beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased, in whom I have glorified my name---hear ye Him.”

            An intensely bright, white figure appeared to materialize in the sky above the Hill Cumorah.  I, along with the entire cast, crew, and audience, gasped.

            At this point in the Book of Mormon story, the Savior was supposed to appear.  And, just as Samuel had predicted a few moments ago, He had, in reality, appeared.  Dressed in dazzlingly white robes, bathed in the light of numerous spotlights, He began his descent.  I had witnessed this spectacle before on previous viewings and had actually been looking forward to it.  It is a very dramatic scene.  And from my experiences with Sister Garr thus far, I even knew that it was Elder Tygart playing the part up there at the top of the Hill Cumorah.  I expected all of this.  These parts had gone exactly as had been planned.

            Instinctively, I looked around for Megan, who had evidently mugged a Nephite woman backstage and taken her apparel, because she was creeping out onto the stage, along with Lauren, who was also now properly arrayed in period attire, toward me, both of them staring up into the heavens.

            “Is your cat gray and white?” Higgs, Jr. had asked.

            “Yes!” Piper had answered.

            “I think he’s up at the top of the hill, wandering around the Moroni statue.”

EPILOGUE

            “This is the two-hundredth semi-annual General Conference of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints...” said the announcer’s voice over the ceiling speakers from within our ward’s chapel.

            “Hurry up!  It’s starting!” I called to the thunder of running feet coming up the sidewalk behind me.  I was holding the outside door open and counting kids.  I ran out of fingers.

            “Just made it, didn’t we?” Megan asked, sliding into the pew ahead of me.  The bishop looked over at us and smiled patiently as our noisy crowd gathered into the center section of pews.

            “Shhh,” shushed Lauren to her four sons.  They immediately complied.

            “Okay, everyone get their conference scavenger sheets out,” Marianna whispered to three little girls dressed in airy pink dresses, each with a different pair of loud colored shoes.  All three pulled out pastel-colored spiral notebooks with feathery pens and pencils.

            “Hey, Dad.  You made it!” whispered Easton, waving with his elbow, trying to keep a sleeping baby level.

            “Here, Piper.  You guys sit here on this side,” suggested Megan, patting the spot on the pew to her left for Piper and her new husband to sit.

            “Brothers and Sisters,” the prophet greeted from behind a rough-hewn wooden lectern perched on what I recognized as the table in the front room of the Whitmer’s log home.  “We extend to you a warm greeting from here within the log home on the grounds of the Peter Whitmer Farm in historic Fayette, New York.  This sacred edifice, with its humble dimensions...”

            “Bampa?” said one of Marianna’s little girls, her head peeking over the top of the pew’s seat-back in front of us.

            “Yes?” I whispered.

            “Mommy says you bumped your head in that house,” she said, pointing a little finger toward the projected image of the prophet.  “Does it still hurt?”

            “No, it’s all better now,” I smiled back.

            “Is that why you don’t have any hair left?” she asked.

            “No, ask your Mom where it all went,” I answered.

            “...Following the opening prayer, the assembled choir will favor us with a song, followed by our first speaker...”

            “What?” she answered quietly.

            “Wouldn’t it be funny if we were called on a mission to give tours there?” I teased.

            She smiled and then we went back to watching our kids’ kids watching conference, some swinging their feet, and at least one sleeping.

            “Brothers and Sisters, we shall now be pleased to welcome Brother and Sister Willard and Beulah Rindlisbacher---“

            “Huh?” all four of our kids, Megan and I said in unison.

            “---to help us open the time capsule created during the one hundred fiftieth celebration...”

            “I wonder why they didn’t just use the word ‘sesquicentennial’,” Piper wondered out loud.

            The video feed immediately switched to the grassy area in front of the log home, where a small group of people were gathered around a pedestal topped with a bronze sculpture depicting the restoration of the priesthood to Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery.  On either side of the pedestal was a cloth-covered table and a simple lectern with a microphone.  Seated in the center behind the pedestal was an ancient-looking couple: the Rindlisbachers, still dressed as they were the last time we’d seen them.

            “Hey, isn’t that the m&m guy?” asked Marianna.

Grandin building by administering the Heimlich maneuver.  Elder Evans explained that following the conclusion of the sesquicentennial celebration of the Church in 1980, this time capsule had been assembled and placed in the Whitmer Farm visitor’s center.  It was scheduled to be opened fifty years later, and that due to the fact that the Rindlisbachers had spent so many years as a missionary couple in the Palmyra area, that the privilege to open the capsule was being extended to them.

            “This is a photograph of President Spencer W. Kimball along with other attendees at the dedication of the Whitmer Farm on April 6th, 1980,” he read.

            “...don’t see anything more on the list, do you?  ...suppose...omitted by accident?” we heard the technicians discussing.

            “...could be an exciting new discovery!” said another technician’s voice.

I wanted to give this coupon to you because you are a nice man and ice cream is good.  Thank you for being the prophet and telling everyone to choose the right.  Your friend, Piper.’”
